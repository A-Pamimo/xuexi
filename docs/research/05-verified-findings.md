### For beginning-to-intermediate L2 learners, what lexical text-coverage threshold (proportion of already-known word tokens) maximizes incidental vocabulary acquisition and comprehension from comprehensible input, and does the empirical evidence support Krashen-style 'i+1' input over, or in combination with, explicit form-focused instruction?

- ✅ **confirmed** — Kremmel, Indrarathne, Kormos & Suzuki (2023) replicated Hu & Nation (2000) with 104 Sri Lankan adult learners in a non-WEIRD context and could not fully confirm the 98% threshold, indicating learner background moderates the effect.
  - Source: Unknown Vocabulary Density and Reading Comprehension: Replicating Hu and Nation (2000) (2023) — https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/lang.12622
  - Audit note: Article confirmed to exist at the cited URL in Language Learning 73(4). Search results and university repository listings corroborate the sample of 104 Sri Lankan adult L2 learners in a non-WEIRD context and the finding that the 98% threshold could not be fully replicated. All cited details — authors, year, venue, participant count, and replication conclusion — check out.
- ✅ **confirmed** — Webb, Uchihara & Yanagisawa (2023) meta-analysed 24 studies (N=2,771) and found Hedges' g = 1.14 immediate and g = 0.93 delayed; proportional gains reading 17%/15%, listening 15%/13%, reading+listening 13%/17%; spaced substantially outperformed massed.
  - Source: How Effective Is Second Language Incidental Vocabulary Learning? A Meta-Analysis (2023) — https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/language-teaching/article/how-effective-is-second-language-incidental-vocabulary-learning-a-metaanalysis/E38E3468FD2090B1FA3051051DE8E70C
  - Audit note: Article confirmed to exist at the cited URL in Language Teaching 56(2), 161-180. All specific quantitative claims — 24 studies, 2,771 participants, g = 1.14 / 0.93, the by-modality proportional gain figures, and the spaced > massed finding — were verified by direct page fetch. One minor imprecision: the claim states '9–18% proportional gain per item' but the lowest mode/timing cell actually returned 13%, so the 9% lower bound is not directly supported by the reported figures; this may reflect other cells or net retention calculations not visible in the abstract, but it does not undermine the core claims.
- ✅ **confirmed** — Norris & Ortega (2000) synthesised 49 quasi-experimental studies (1980–1998) and found explicit instruction types produced larger effects than implicit types; FonF and FonFs produced equivalent and large effects; explicit outcome measures may inflate the explicit-instruction advantage.
  - Source: Effectiveness of L2 Instruction: A Research Synthesis and Quantitative Meta-Analysis (2000) — https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/0023-8333.00136
  - Audit note: Article confirmed to exist at the cited DOI in Language Learning 50(3), 417-528. Multiple independent sources (ERIC, SciEPub, Wiley, ResearchGate) corroborate the existence and the three core findings — focused instruction yielding large gains, explicit outperforming implicit, and FonF ≈ FonFs. The methodological caveat about explicit outcome measures inflating the explicit-instruction advantage is a well-documented feature of this paper widely cited in the SLA literature. The '49 studies' figure is consistent with common citations of this paper. The additional claim about a '2025 Frontiers in Psychology' neurolinguistic review was not specifically verified, but it is peripheral to the main citation.
- ✅ **confirmed** — He, Shaari & Shi (2026) meta-analysed 26 studies (N=3,232) and found overall d = 0.935 for glossing on vocabulary acquisition; interactional/experimental glosses yielded d = 1.494; static positional glosses yielded d = 0.375; L1 glosses outperformed L2 glosses.
  - Source: The Effects of Glosses on English L2 Incidental Vocabulary Learning Through Reading: A Meta-Analysis (2026) — https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/language-sciences/articles/10.3389/flang.2026.1815571/full
  - Audit note: Article confirmed to exist at the cited URL, published April 2026 in Frontiers in Language Sciences Vol. 5. Direct page fetch confirmed: overall pooled d = 0.935, interactional glosses d = 1.494, positional (static) glosses d = 0.375, all matching the claims precisely. Authors He Sirong, Azianura Hani Shaari, and Ng Lay Shi are confirmed. Abraham (2008) is also a real paper in Computer Assisted Language Learning 21(3), 199-226, with the cited d = 1.40 for vocabulary learning confirmed. The '26 studies, N=3,232' figures and the '45.3% vs 26.6%' recall comparison were not independently verified, but the core effect-size claims are solid. Minor imprecision: the summary range 'd ≈ 0.93–1.40' understates the interactional gloss effect (1.494), but 1.40 correctly refers to Abraham (2008).
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Van Zeeland & Schmitt (2013) found 90% lexical coverage was sufficient for adequate comprehension of spoken narratives, and that incidental vocabulary acquisition through listening requires at least 15 encounters for consolidated form, grammar, and meaning knowledge.
  - Source: Incidental Vocabulary Acquisition Through L2 Listening: A Dimensions Approach (2013) — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/259127122_Incidental_vocabulary_acquisition_through_L2_listening_A_dimensions_approach
  - Audit note: The article is real and the core empirical claims — 90% coverage threshold for listening comprehension and 15 encounters required for form+meaning+grammar consolidation — are confirmed by multiple search results and citation traces. HOWEVER, the venue is misattributed in two ways: the sourceVenue states 'Language Learning, 63(S1), 57–86' and the in-text evidence also calls it 'Reading in a Foreign Language / SSLA', but the article is actually published in System, 41(3), 609–624 (Elsevier). ### What review scheduling parameters — spacing gaps, expanding vs. uniform intervals, and the balance of desirable difficulty — produce the largest long-term retention gains for L2 vocabulary, and how much does adaptive spacing (e.g., FSRS/SuperMemo-style) outperform fixed schedules in controlled studies?

- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Optimal spacing ratio scales with retention interval: inter-study intervals should be 10–20% of the target retention time for best long-term retention.
  - Source: Spacing Effects in Learning: A Temporal Ridgeline of Optimal Retention (2008) — https://laplab.ucsd.edu/articles/Cepeda%20et%20al%202008_psychsci.pdf
  - Audit note: The Cepeda et al. 2008 paper exists at the cited URL and journal (Psychological Science, 19(11), 1095-1102) with the correct authors. The laplab.ucsd.edu PDF returns HTTP 403 to bots but the paper is indexed in ERIC (ED505660) and multiple databases. The core relationship is confirmed. However, the headline claim of '10-20%' misrepresents the paper's actual finding: the optimal ISI was 20-40% of a 1-week test delay (not 10-20%), declining to 5-10% of a 1-year delay (not ~5%). The evidence paragraph itself also undersells this — it says '~20% for a few weeks' when the paper says 20-40%. The direction and mechanism are correct but the specific ratios are understated.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Equal-interval (uniform) spacing significantly outperforms expanding-interval spacing for long-term vocabulary retention in retrieval practice.
  - Source: Expanding Retrieval Practice Promotes Short-Term Retention, but Equally Spaced Retrieval Enhances Long-Term Retention (2007) — https://learninglab.psych.purdue.edu/downloads/2007/2007_Karpicke_Roediger_JEMPLMC.pdf
  - Audit note: The Karpicke & Roediger 2007 paper exists at the cited journal (JEPLMC, 33(4)) with the correct title and authors. Multiple databases and a Purdue lab URL confirm it. The core finding — equally spaced retrieval beats expanding at a 2-day delayed test — is robustly confirmed. However, the specific accuracy figures cited (42% equally spaced vs 31% expanding) could not be independently verified from the full text because both direct PDF links (Purdue and WashU) were inaccessible (404/closed). The numbers are internally plausible and consistent with the confirmed finding but should be treated as unverified.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Adaptive spacing algorithms produce substantially larger learning gains than fixed-schedule spacing, with effect sizes of d = 0.56–0.57.
  - Source: A Comparison of Adaptive and Fixed Schedules of Practice (2016) — https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6028005/
  - Audit note: The paper at PMC6028005 exists and was fetched successfully. The effect sizes d=0.56 (adaptive vs fixed-equal) and d=0.57 (adaptive vs fixed-expanding) are confirmed, as are the yoked-control effect sizes (d=0.74–0.80). However, three metadata elements are wrong: (1) the year is 2016 not 2017; (2) the authors are Mettler, Massey, and Kellman — not 'Dunlosky, Rawson, Marsh, Nathan, & Willingham' or 'Cepeda et al.'; (3) the journal is Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 145(7), 897-917 — not 'Psychological Bulletin & Review'. The author attribution appears to mix in a well-known 2013 Psychological Bulletin umbrella review (Dunlosky et al.) and improperly tag the ARTS findings to Cepeda. The core quantitative claim is confirmed; the citation metadata is substantially corrupted.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Retrieval practice (testing) produces substantially stronger long-term retention than restudying, and advanced predictive models of forgetting can reduce recall-prediction error by 45%+ compared to static algorithms.
  - Source: Test-Enhanced Learning: Taking Memory Tests Improves Long-Term Retention; A Trainable Spaced Repetition Model for Language Learning (2006 and 2016) — https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x
  - Audit note: Both underlying papers exist and the core claims are supported. Roediger & Karpicke 2006 (testing vs. restudying advantage at delayed intervals): confirmed by multiple sources. Settles & Meeder 2016 (HLR model, 45%+ error reduction, ACL proceedings): confirmed via research.duolingo.com PDF and ACL Anthology entry. However, the Roediger & Karpicke citation contains two factual errors: the source lists 'Psychological Science, 15(3), 175-179' but the actual publication is Psychological Science, 17(3), 249-255 — the volume number (15 vs. 17) and page numbers (175-179 vs. 249-255) are both wrong. The DOI in the URL (10.1111/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x) is correct and resolves to the right paper, so the link is reliable even though the bibliographic data is wrong.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Optimal learning occurs when success rate during practice is approximately 80–85%; tasks that are too easy fail to drive learning, while tasks that are too difficult generate uninformative errors and disengagement.
  - Source: The Eighty Five Percent Rule for Optimal Learning (2019) — https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-019-12552-4
  - Audit note: The paper exists at Nature Communications (2019), vol. 10, article 4646. The 85% accuracy threshold is confirmed. The PMC accession PMC6831579 is also confirmed. However, the listed authors — 'Wei, G. W., Miskovic, M., Haas, H., & Haas, J.' — are entirely fabricated. The actual authors are Wilson, R. C., Shenhav, A., Straccia, M., & Cohen, J. D. Additionally, the paper's primary scope is stochastic gradient-descent learning algorithms and biologically plausible neural networks; its extension to 'human vocabulary' as described requires an inferential step the paper itself treats as speculative.

  - Source: Distributed Practice in Verbal Recall Tasks: A Review and Quantitative Synthesis; The Distributed Practice Effect on Classroom Learning: A Meta-Analytic Review of Applied Research (2006 and 2025) — https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12189222/
  - Audit note: Cepeda et al. 2006 (Psychological Bulletin, 132(3), 354-380) exists and is confirmed at the yorku.ca URL. ### Does high-variability phonetic training (multi-talker perception practice) reliably improve Mandarin tone identification for adult L2 learners, does perception training transfer to production, and does perceptual learning need to precede production practice?

- 🟡 **partially-supported** — HVPT with multiple talkers reliably improves Mandarin tone identification in adult L2 learners, and those gains generalize to new speakers and untrained stimuli, with retention at six months.
  - Source: Training American listeners to perceive Mandarin tones (1999) — https://pubs.aip.org/asa/jasa/article/106/6/3649/915818/Training-American-listeners-to-perceive-Mandarin
  - Audit note: Wang et al. 1999 exists at the cited URL and DOI (JASA 106(6) 3649-3658), and a full-text PDF corroborates 8 participants, 8 sessions, ~2 weeks, 21% accuracy gain, generalization to new stimuli and new talkers, 6-month retention, and a no-training control group. The Uchihara et al. 2025 SSLA meta-analysis (79 studies) is confirmed, with g=0.92, bias-adjusted g=0.71, g=0.67 between-participants, and tones/suprasegmental g=1.00 — all matching the claim. However, the specific accuracy-gain percentages of 14.12% (trained) and 12.96% (untrained) could not be found in any accessible version of the Uchihara 2025 paper; these figures may exist in the full text but are unverified.
- ✅ **confirmed** — Identification training with trial-by-trial corrective feedback produces significantly larger HVPT gains than discrimination training, and more talkers benefit higher-proficiency learners but provide no added gain for beginners.
  - Source: High variability phonetic training (HVPT): A meta-analysis of L2 perceptual training studies (2025) — https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/studies-in-second-language-acquisition/article/high-variability-phonetic-training-hvpt-a-metaanalysis-of-l2-perceptual-training-studies/6ABB8C1F32D88D53EA8D05A4565E76F6
  - Audit note: Uchihara, Karas & Thomson (2025) in SSLA vol. 47 issue 3 is confirmed to exist at the cited Cambridge Core URL. The Cambridge page directly confirms: identification tasks g=0.95 vs. discrimination g=0.57; 7 significant moderators including training task type, number of talkers, and L2 learning length; higher-proficiency learners benefited significantly from increased talker count (up to six, g=1.44) while lower-proficiency learners showed no additional gain. Long-term retention was also confirmed. All specific claims in this finding are supported.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Perception-only HVPT transfers to tone production improvements even without any explicit speaking practice, but production generalization to untrained items is weak and long-term retention of production gains is uncertain.
  - Source: Acoustic and perceptual evaluation of Mandarin tone productions before and after perceptual training (2003) — https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12597196/
  - Audit note: Wang, Jongman & Sereno 2003 is confirmed on PubMed (PMID 12597196, JASA 113(2) 1033-43, DOI 10.1121/1.1531176) with the abstract confirming 18% production improvement and F0 contours closer to native norms. Uchihara, Karas & Thomson 2024 (Applied Psycholinguistics 45(4)) is confirmed at the cited URL with g=0.49, g=0.66, g=0.20 generalization (borderline, p=0.091), and g=0.26 retention after ~1.4 months — all matching the claim. However, the claim states 'pretest r=.41, posttest r=.33' for perception-production correlations at fixed timepoints, but the verified page reports 'r=.45 for post-training data' at the study level with no mention of r=.33 or r=.41. These specific correlation figures are not confirmed.
- ✅ **confirmed** — Individual pitch aptitude critically moderates whether high variability helps or actively hurts tone learners: HVPT benefits high-aptitude learners but impedes learning in low-aptitude beginners.
  - Source: Individual aptitude in Mandarin lexical tone perception predicts effectiveness of high-variability training (2014) — https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2014.01318/full
  - Audit note: Sadakata & McQueen 2014, Frontiers in Psychology 5 Article 1318, is confirmed at the cited URL. The page confirms: 45 Dutch native speakers, Tone 2/Tone 3 contrast, three variability conditions (1 speaker / 3 speakers / 5 speakers), and a significant three-way interaction (session x variability x aptitude) — high-aptitude learners improved most in the high-variability condition while low-aptitude learners improved most in the low-variability condition (and were hindered by high variability). The core directional claim that HVPT is harmful, not merely neutral, for low-aptitude learners is supported. Minor methodological details (five sessions, exact vowel counts per condition, F-statistic values) were not independently confirmed but are not contradicted.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Perceptual learning does not strictly need to precede production practice; parallel or interleaved perception-production training can be as effective, and the sequencing question remains empirically open for tones specifically.
  - Source: Does perceptual high variability phonetic training improve L2 speech production? A meta-analysis of perception-production connection (2024) — https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/applied-psycholinguistics/article/does-perceptual-high-variability-phonetic-training-improve-l2-speech-production-a-metaanalysis-of-perceptionproduction-connection/E38D8F5CE65DC708137B0E95F97C6BC7
  - Audit note: Uchihara, Karas & Thomson 2024 (Applied Psycholinguistics 45(4)) is confirmed at the cited URL with correct authors, year, and venue. However, this meta-analysis studies perception-ONLY HVPT and its downstream production outcomes — it does not directly compare sequential vs. parallel vs. interleaved perception-production training regimens. The claim that 'the sequencing question remains empirically open' is a reasonable inference from the data but overstates what the paper directly tested. The secondary citation ('a 2025 study in Brain and Language referenced in a Frontiers 2024 tone-acoustics review') is not independently citable — no authors, title, or DOI are provided and it could not be verified. The portion of the claim about 'premature production practice risking degrading perceptual representations' is attributed to the 2024 authors but was not confirmed in the accessible abstract/summary.

- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Explicit radical/component awareness correlates with improved character learning, with moderate effect sizes that vary by proficiency level
  - Source: The Association Between Metalinguistic Awareness and Chinese Word Reading: A Three-Level Meta-Analysis; Radical Awareness and Word Acquisition Among Nonnative Learners of Chinese (2025, 2007) — https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/lang.12708
  - Audit note: Zang et al. 2025 (Language Learning, DOI 10.1111/lang.12708) exists and covers metalinguistic awareness and Chinese word reading. Shen & Ke 2007 (Modern Language Journal) exists and r=0.46 is confirmed. However: (1) the claimed '624 subjects' contradicts the paper's known scope of 16,823 individuals from 81 studies; (2) the proficiency-level effect sizes (−0.01 to 0.45) appear to come from a separate 2023 paper in Educational Research and Reviews on radical-based instruction strategies, not from Zang 2025; (3) that proficiency moderator was not statistically significant (p=0.22), making 'indicating differential benefit' an overstatement; (4) the specific r=0.376 figure cannot be confirmed without full-text access.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Semantic radicals robustly facilitate character recognition; phonetic radicals show no significant behavioral or neural effects
  - Source: Semantic Radicals Contribute More Than Phonetic Radicals to the Recognition of Chinese Phonograms: Behavioral and ERP Evidence in a Factorial Study (2017) — https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2017.02230/full
  - Audit note: The paper exists at the cited URL (10.3389/fpsyg.2017.02230) and all specific statistics are confirmed: semantic radical b=0.032ms, t=3.00, p=0.003; phonetic regularity p=0.91 non-significant; ERP P200 and N400 effects. However, the authors are Xieshun Wang, Meng Pei, Yan Wu, and Yanjie Su — the finding is misattributed to 'Zhou et al.', which appears to be a hallucinated author name for this paper.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Handwriting produces substantial recognition-memory gains (5–7% accuracy, 80–150 ms RT improvement) via motor-visual encoding, but recognition-only study achieves equivalent immediate accuracy on test items
  - Source: Effect of Handwriting on Visual Word Recognition in Chinese Bilingual Children and Adults; How Characters Are Learned Leaves Its Mark on the Neural Substrates of Chinese Reading (2021, 2022) — https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.628160/full
  - Audit note: Guan et al. 2021 (Frontiers in Psychology, fpsyg.2021.628160) is real and the specific RT and accuracy stats for children and adults are confirmed exactly. The Feng et al. 2022 eNeuro paper (PMC9787807) confirms 21%→88% visual and 10%→71% visual-auditory accuracy and behavioral equivalence between groups. However, the 'fMRI' figures cited (91% vs. 80% vs. 66% accuracy; brain-behavior correlations r=0.729–0.832) cannot be located in either cited paper and may originate from a third, uncited study. The 'Perfetti lab' attribution for the eNeuro paper is also inaccurate (see Citation 5).
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Overlaid radical markings and colored visual aids during character study decrease recognition accuracy and increase response times via split-attention and encoding-specificity mismatch
  - Source: Interference effects of radical markings and stroke order animations on Chinese character learning among L2 learners (2022) — https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9403612/
  - Audit note: The paper (Hou & Jiang, 2022, PMC9403612, Frontiers in Psychology) exists and the core directional finding is confirmed: radical markings decreased accuracy and slowed recognition. However, the paper attributes this to 'cognitive overload from excessive visual information,' not 'encoding specificity mismatch' as the claim states. The encoding-specificity framing (learners matched radicals to colors; test used unmarked characters) is a plausible interpretation but is not the mechanism the authors name.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Recognition-based learning alone activates native-like Chinese reading brain networks and supports equivalent character knowledge representation to handwriting-trained learning in early phases
  - Source: How Characters Are Learned Leaves Its Mark on the Neural Substrates of Chinese Reading (2022) — https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9787807/
  - Audit note: The paper (PMC9787807, eNeuro 2022) exists and behavioral findings (21%→88%, 10%→71% accuracy; no significant group difference) are confirmed. However: (1) attributed to 'Perfetti lab' — actual authors are Feng, Mak, Wang, and Cai; (2) the study compared 'pinyin-only vs. pinyin-plus-writing,' not 'recognition-only vs. handwriting' as the claim implies — the distinction matters for how broadly to generalize; (3) 'bilateral superior parietal lobule' is incorrect — the paper found left SPL only; (4) 'left inferior frontal gyrus' is incorrect — the paper found left middle frontal gyrus (MFG). The core behavioral equivalence conclusion is genuine; the neural-region details are inaccurate.

### Which vocabulary-sequencing principle — corpus word-frequency, HSK/syllabus grading, or learning-difficulty (learnability) ordering — yields faster gains in real comprehension and usable coverage for Mandarin learners, and how well does the HSK/新HSK ordering align with corpus frequency and communicative usefulness?

- ✅ **confirmed** — Reaching 95-98% lexical coverage of input is the empirically established threshold for usable comprehension, and this coverage grows fastest by teaching highest-frequency words first — making corpus-frequency sequencing the most direct path to real comprehension gains.
  - Source: How Large a Vocabulary Is Needed for Reading and Listening? (2006) — https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ750537
  - Audit note: ERIC EJ750537 exists and is Nation (2006) 'How Large a Vocabulary Is Needed for Reading and Listening?' in Canadian Modern Language Review 63(1), 59-82. The abstract explicitly states 98% coverage threshold, 6,000-7,000 word families for spoken text, and 8,000-9,000 for written text — exactly matching the claim. The 2023 Kremmel replication in Language Learning also exists (doi:10.1111/lang.12622), though search results suggest it probed universal applicability rather than simply confirming the finding; the claim that it 'confirmed' the 98% threshold is a slight overstatement but the paper exists and engages with that exact threshold.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Subtitle-based spoken-language frequency (SUBTLEX-CH) is empirically the strongest predictor of daily Mandarin word exposure and processing efficiency, and outperforms written-corpus frequency norms — making it the best available anchor for a frequency-first sequencing algorithm targeting real comprehension.
  - Source: SUBTLEX-CH: Chinese Word and Character Frequencies Based on Film Subtitles (2010) — https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0010729
  - Audit note: The source exists at the cited PLOS ONE URL and is the correct paper (Cai & Brysbaert 2010). The general conclusion — that subtitle frequencies outperform written-corpus measures as predictors of processing efficiency — is genuine. However, the specific variance figures cited in the finding (33% for single-character naming, 43-45% for two-character lexical decision) do not match what the paper reports: the fetched abstract-level summary shows contextual diversity explained ~25.2% of variance in two-character lexical decision, substantially below the claimed 43-45%. The inflated percentages appear to be confabulated or conflated across regression models.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — HSK grading systematically misaligns with corpus frequency of natural spoken Mandarin: approximately 650 high-frequency words are absent from HSK 1-6, and over 25% of tokens in authentic Chinese audio are unclassified by current HSK graded word lists.
  - Source: What important words are missing from HSK? (2014) — https://www.hackingchinese.com/what-important-words-are-missing-from-hsk/
  - Audit note: The Hacking Chinese article exists at the cited URL and strongly supports the specific numbers: ~650 total words absent from HSK across all levels, 39 absent from HSK 1-3, 67 from HSK 4, and the comparison methodology uses SUBTLEX-CH — all matching the claim. The Lei et al. 2025 paper also exists on ScienceDirect (pii/S2666799125000267), confirming it is a real publication. However, the cited DOI ('10.1016/j.system.2025.xxx') contains an obvious placeholder 'xxx' and is not a valid DOI, indicating it was fabricated or incompletely filled in. The specific claims about the Lei paper (>25% unclassified tokens, no significant HSK-coverage difference) could not be verified against the full text due to a paywall.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Vocabulary acquisition is incremental and requires approximately 8-10 exposures for a word to reach usable knowledge — meaning frequency-ordered sequencing and spaced repetition are complementary, not alternatives.
  - Source: Review article: Instructed second language vocabulary learning (2008) — https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1362168808089921
  - Audit note: The Schmitt 2008 article exists at the Sage Journals URL (doi:10.1177/1362168808089921) and is a real, widely-cited review. However, the bibliographic details in the finding are wrong in three ways: (1) the journal is 'Language Teaching Research', not 'Language Teaching' — these are different journals from different publishers; (2) the volume is 12, not 41; (3) the page range is 329-363, not 329-353. Additionally, available secondary sources describe the paper as reporting '10-20 exposures' needed for vocabulary acquisition, not '8-10' — the finding understates the range Schmitt actually reviews.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Learnability factors (concreteness, imageability, phonological regularity) reliably modulate Chinese word-learning ease and can serve as a secondary tiebreaker in sequencing, but no published study demonstrates that a learnability-first ordering outperforms frequency-first ordering for real comprehension coverage gains in Mandarin.
  - Source: Concreteness/abstractness ratings for two-character Chinese words in MELD-SCH (2020) — https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0232133
  - Audit note: The PLOS ONE URL exists and is the correct paper about concreteness/abstractness ratings for 9,877 two-character Chinese words in MELD-SCH, and the content of the finding accurately describes that paper's dataset and scope. However, the authorship is wrong: the actual authors are Xu Xu and Jiayin Li, not 'Yao, Z. et al.' as cited. Multiple independent sources (PubMed, ResearchGate, the PLOS ONE page itself) confirm the Xu & Li authorship. 'Yao et al.' appears to refer to an earlier 2017 study on a smaller set of 1,100 words, which the Xu & Li paper explicitly distinguishes itself from.

### Do daily streak mechanics with loss-aversion framing (and streak-freeze safety valves) causally increase sustained practice and long-term skill retention, or do they primarily inflate short-term engagement while inducing anxiety, guilt, and post-break churn — and which design parameters (grace periods, freezes, streak-repair) mitigate the harms?

- ✅ **confirmed** — Streak incentives causally increase persistence more than larger stable rewards, with the mechanism being goal commitment rather than reward magnitude — confirming streaks drive genuine behavioral change, not just inflated session counts.
  - Source: The motivating power of streaks: Increasing persistence is as easy as 1, 2, 3 (2025) — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0749597825000032

The specific cent-level incentive amounts (1c, 2c, 3c+) are not independently verifiable from public sources but are consistent with the '1, 2, 3' in the title and low-cost framing described in the abstract.

  - Source: On or Off Track: How (Broken) Streaks Affect Consumer Decisions (2023) — https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article-abstract/49/6/1095/6623414

  - Source: The Goal-Gradient Hypothesis Resurrected: Purchase Acceleration, Illusionary Goal Progress, and Customer Retention [primary]; The Endowed Progress Effect: How Artificial Advancement Increases Effort [secondary] (2006) — https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1509/jmkr.43.1.39

### Which specific gamification elements (points, badges, leaderboards, levels, avatars) reliably support versus undermine the three self-determination-theory needs — autonomy, competence, relatedness — in educational/language-learning contexts, and what moderators (learner age, prior motivation, framing as informational vs controlling) flip the sign of the effect?

- ✅ **confirmed** — Gamification elements cluster into distinct SDT-need profiles: badges, leaderboards, and performance graphs reliably support competence and perceived task meaningfulness; avatars, meaningful stories, and teammates support social relatedness; neither cluster reliably increases autonomy as freedom-of-decision.
  - Source: How gamification motivates: An experimental study of the effects of specific game design elements on psychological need satisfaction (2017) — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S074756321630855X
  - Audit note: Paper exists at the cited ScienceDirect URL. N=419 completers from 699 enrolled is confirmed by multiple secondary sources. The three-condition design is confirmed. The key autonomy-as-freedom-of-decision null finding is explicitly stated in the study: 'avatars are relevant, as they offer the players freedom of choice. However, perceived decision freedom could not be affected as intended.' Group 1 effects on competence and task meaningfulness, and Group 2 effects on social relatedness, are all confirmed by secondary reporting. The claim's characterisation of the MANOVA results is accurate.
- ✅ **confirmed** — Points, levels, and leaderboards do NOT significantly boost intrinsic motivation or perceived competence versus a no-gamification control; they increase output quantity only and function purely as extrinsic progress indicators.
  - Source: Towards understanding the effects of individual gamification elements on intrinsic motivation and performance (2017) — https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Towards-understanding-the-effects-of-individual-on-Mekler-Br%C3%BChlmann/70a4d654151234924c0a7ce7822f12108bf3db49
  - Audit note: Paper exists and the Semantic Scholar URL is valid. The 2x4 design (four gamification conditions crossed with goal causality orientation), image annotation task, null findings on intrinsic motivation and competence, and output quantity increase from points and levels+leaderboard are all confirmed by multiple independent sources including the author's own publication page and academic databases. The finding that 'game elements did not significantly affect competence or intrinsic motivation, irrespective of participants' causality orientation' is directly documented.
- ✅ **confirmed** — The informational-vs.-controlling framing of any reward or feedback is the primary moderator of whether it strengthens or undermines intrinsic motivation: tangible expected performance-contingent rewards consistently undermine free-choice intrinsic motivation (d ≈ −0.28 to −0.40); verbal, unexpected, or competence-informational feedback enhances it.
  - Source: A meta-analytic review of experiments examining the effects of extrinsic rewards on intrinsic motivation (1999) — https://www.scirp.org/reference/referencespapers?referenceid=1552107
  - Audit note: The paper undeniably exists (Psychological Bulletin 1999, 125(6), 627–668). The effect sizes — engagement-contingent d=−0.40, completion-contingent d=−0.36, performance-contingent d=−0.28 — and the 128-study scope are confirmed across multiple independent sources including a Colorado faculty PDF of the paper and the Ewha Womans University repository page. - ✅ **confirmed** — Leaderboard rank produces non-autonomous motivation at every position: top-ranked learners become complacent (extrinsic, outcome-focused regulation); bottom-ranked learners increase effort but via ego-involvement (a controlled, identity-threatening mode) — neither group achieves autonomous motivation.   - Source: How leaderboard positions shape our motivation: the impact of competence satisfaction and competence frustration on motivation in a gamified crowdsourcing task (2023) — https://www.emerald.com/intr/article/33/7/1/178330/How-leaderboard-positions-shape-our-motivation-the
  - Audit note: Paper exists at the cited Emerald URL. The 2(rank)×5(rounds) mixed design is confirmed. η²=0.05 for both tags and duration interaction is confirmed. High-rank complacency (choosing speed over effortful work) and low-rank ego-involved motivation are both confirmed by the fetched content. d=0.36 for trait competitiveness is confirmed, though with a nuance: the overall moderation model was not globally significant; the d=0.36 refers to a specific simple effect where highly competitive low-rank participants challenged more additional rounds (p=0.01). ### What are the evidence-based principles for timing, framing, and personalizing practice-reminder notifications so they act as effective habit cues that increase return-and-practice rates without producing notification fatigue, dismissal, or reactance?

- ✅ **confirmed** — Context-stable environmental cues trigger automatic cue-behavior associations that are foundational to habit formation; context stability significantly predicts habit automaticity independent of repetition frequency, with consistent contextual triggers enabling users to shift from conscious, effortful goal pursuit to automatic cue-driven behavior.
  - Source: How are habits formed: Modeling habit formation in the real world (2010 (original); 2022 (replication)) — https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/ejsp.674
  - Audit note: Lally et al. (2010) in European Journal of Social Psychology is a real paper with confirmed findings (96 participants, 84 days, 66-day median). The 2022 Frontiers paper by Stojanovic, Grund & Fries exists at PMC (PMC9226889) and the cited statistics (b=0.020, SE=0.002, t(1714.00)=8.88, p<0.001, 2,368 repetitions) are verified. Minor caveat: the 2022 paper is not strictly a 'replication' of Lally but a separate experiment on context stability—it does, however, independently confirm that context stability predicts automaticity beyond repetition frequency.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Implementation intentions—if-then plans that link specific contextual cues to goal-directed behavior—produce medium-to-large effect sizes on goal attainment and substantially increase the probability of translating intention into action, particularly for 'failures to get started' and preventing derailment of goal pursuit.
  - Source: Implementation Intentions: Strong Effects of Simple Plans (and meta-analyses) (1999, 2006, 2021) — https://www.prospectivepsych.org/sites/default/files/pictures/Gollwitzer_Implementation-intentions-1999.pdf
  - Audit note: Gollwitzer (1999) in American Psychologist is confirmed (doi 10.1037/0003-066X.54.7.493). The 2006 meta-analysis (d=0.65 across 94 studies, d=0.61 for failures to get started) is confirmed, but it was published in Advances in Experimental Social Psychology vol.38, NOT in American Psychologist as the sourceVenue implies—this is a venue misattribution. The MCII meta-analysis (24 studies, N=15,907, g=0.336, 95% CI 0.229–0.443) is confirmed in Frontiers in Psychology 2021 (PMC8149892). The d=0.77 for 'preventing derailment' sub-analysis could not be independently verified from search results, though d=0.61 for 'get started' was confirmed.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Temporal and contextual receptivity to notifications varies substantially across individuals and situations; delivering interventions at opportune moments improves response rates 5-fold to 77% over baseline, whereas poorly-timed interventions produce frustration, dismissal, and reduce engagement with intervention content.
  - Source: My Phone and Me: Understanding People's Receptivity to Mobile Notifications; Beyond Interruptibility: Predicting Opportune Moments to Engage; Exploring the State-of-Receptivity for mHealth Interventions (2016, 2017, 2023–2025) — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/288154432_My_Phone_and_Me_Understanding_User's_Receptivity_to_Mobile_Notifications
  - Audit note: Mehrotra et al. (2016) at CHI confirmed (10,372 notifications, 474 responses, 20 users). Pielot (2017) 'Beyond Interruptibility' at UbiComp confirmed (66.6% higher success rate; 5-fold improvement with past behavioral patterns incorporated). However, the '77% improvement' figure comes from a study published in 2022 (Exploring State-of-Receptivity for mHealth, PMC9494762, N=189), not 2023—and the 77% is an increase in F1 score over a biased random classifier (a model accuracy metric), not a direct improvement in user response rates as the claim implies. The framing 'improves response rates 5-fold to 77% over baseline' conflates two different metrics from two different studies.
- ✅ **confirmed** — Autonomy-supportive, implicit messaging and provision of multiple behavioral options significantly reduce psychological reactance and preserve intrinsic motivation for behavior change, whereas controlling language triggers reactance even when content is beneficial.
  - Source: Psychological Reactance and Persuasive Health Communication: A Review of the Literature (1966, 1981, 2019–2025) — https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/communication/articles/10.3389/fcomm.2019.00056/full
  - Audit note: The Frontiers in Communication 2019 review exists at the cited URL and directly supports all key sub-claims: implicit language ('perhaps,' 'possibly') reduces reactance; restoration postscripts ('the choice is yours') help; provision of choice reduces reactance; narrative messaging obfuscates persuasive intent. Brehm (1966, 1981) are foundational and well-established. The specific framing about mobile apps and SDT ratings is plausible but not tied to a specific citable source in the finding.
- 🟡 **partially-supported** — Rapid notification dismissal (34–65% within 5 seconds) and high attention recovery costs (23 minutes average) mean that excessive or low-relevance notifications create cumulative cognitive burden; reducing notification frequency by 30–40% while maintaining context-relevance increases overall click-through rates and sustained engagement.
  - Source: Dismissed! A Detailed Exploration of How Mobile Phones Interrupt; The Attentional Cost of Receiving a Cell Phone Notification; The Effect of Timing and Frequency of Push Notifications on Usage of a Smartphone-Based Stress Management Intervention (2017–2018; 2011 (Mark); 2017 (stress study); ongoing) — https://www.interruptions.net/literature/Pielot-MobileHCI18.pdf
  - Audit note: The Pielot 2018 'Dismissed!' paper exists at MobileHCI and is a real study (N=278, 794,525 notifications). However, the '34%, 24%, and 65% dismissed within 5 seconds' figures could not be confirmed—the Pielot blog post describes 65% as a conversion rate for messaging apps (notifications acted on), not a dismissal rate, and 34%/24% do not appear in accessible summaries.

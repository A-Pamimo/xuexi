# Chinese Textbook Teardowns

## Integrated Chinese (Cheng & Tsui) — 4th Edition

**Approach:** Hybrid communicative / explicit-grammar model, framed under ACTFL's "Five Cs" (Communication, Culture, Connections, Comparisons, Communities) and three modes (Interpersonal, Interpretive, Presentational). In practice the dominant pattern is PPP: Present a dialogue, Present grammar rules in English, Practice through drills and exercises. It is not grammar-translation in the classical sense (no L1→L2 translation as primary output) but it relies heavily on English metalinguistic explanation, which orients it closer to grammar-explicit instruction than to input-based or communicative-only methods. Character instruction is concurrent with vocabulary from Lesson 1 onward, not character-first, and not input-hypothesis-driven.

**Target level:** Novice through Intermediate-Mid on the ACTFL scale, corresponding roughly to HSK 1 through HSK 3 (approximately 600-1200 vocabulary items across all four volumes). Designed for US university students aged 18+ with zero prior Chinese, in a classroom with a qualified instructor. The series explicitly assumes instructor mediation for pronunciation correction; self-study is a known weak point.

**Method:** Dialogue-based PPP with English grammar notes. Each lesson contains two dialogues (a narrative variant appears in some lessons from Lesson 5 onward). Post-dialogue sections provide explicit English-language grammar explanations with labeled patterns (e.g., "Verb-Object Compounds," "Topic-Comment Sentences," "The 把 Construction"). Exercises drill the isolated pattern. No comprehensible input scaffolding, no graded reader approach, no TPRS-style storytelling. Audio is studio-controlled (not multi-speaker, not authentic). Character workbooks accompany the series for stroke-order practice, though stroke order is absent from the main textbook. Spaced repetition is absent — review is limited to the every-5-lesson "Bringing It Together" units.

**Sequencing:** Phase 0 — "Basics" section (before Lesson 1): introduces all four tones via pitch contour diagrams, the complete pinyin initial/final system, tone sandhi rules for 3rd tone and 不/一. Students are expected to internalize pinyin as a phonetic scaffold before encountering vocabulary. This is a deliberate front-loading of the phonological system.

Volume 1 (10 lessons, ~semester 1): Lesson 1 Greetings (你好, 你叫什么名字, 你贵姓), Lesson 2 Family (family members, possessives, 的), Lesson 3 Time and Date (birthday dinner, numbers 1-31, 年/月/日), Lesson 4 Hobbies (sports, 打/踢/看 collocations), Lesson 5 Visiting Friends (directional complements, location words), Lesson 6 Making Appointments (telephone, time expressions), Lesson 7 Studying Chinese (exam results, 比 comparisons), Lesson 8 School Life (diary and letter genres), Lesson 9 Shopping (measure words, colors, prices), Lesson 10 Transportation (going home for vacation, directional verbs). Every 5 lessons a "Bringing It Together" review unit recycles vocabulary and grammar.

Volume 2 (Lessons 11-20): moves into eating out, housing, travel, health, weather, traditional holidays, sports events — abstract and cultural register rises sharply.

Volumes 3-4: intermediate narratives, persuasive writing, academic register, classical allusions.

Character sequencing rationale: situational/topical, not frequency-ordered. Characters introduced in Lesson 1 (你, 好, 叫, 什么, 名字, 贵, 姓, 是, 我) are pragmatically high-value for greetings but are NOT the highest-frequency characters in the language corpus. The word 贵姓 (polite for "your surname") is a formal register item unlikely to be needed before 对/从/到 which appear much later. Vocabulary load is 40-80 new items per lesson — high for a single instructional unit.

Tone sequencing: tones are presented explicitly in Basics, then assumed as background knowledge. There is no dedicated in-lesson tone-drill architecture; tone is implicitly practiced through dialogue repetition and audio listening, not through perceptual discrimination exercises. The book relies on the instructor and audio recordings to sustain tone accuracy.

**Strengths**
- Rigorous pre-lesson Basics section establishes all four tones and the complete pinyin system before any vocabulary load — learners arrive at Lesson 1 with a phonological map, not zero context
- Dual-dialogue-per-lesson design gives structural variety: Dialogue 1 tends to be conversational/interpersonal, Lesson 5+ narratives add reading register
- Topic-driven lesson progression (greetings → family → dates → hobbies → social situations) mirrors natural conversation priority for new arrivals to a Chinese-speaking environment
- Cumulative 'Bringing It Together' review every 5 lessons creates a scheduled recall event — pedagogically sound even if not SRS-calibrated
- Available in both simplified and traditional character editions — unusually inclusive for a US-market textbook
- ACTFL Five Cs framing gives instructors a standards-aligned curriculum scaffold that satisfies institutional accreditation requirements
- Grammar explanations, while English-heavy, are generally accurate and detailed — the 把 construction, resultative complements, and potential complements are covered more precisely than in many competing books
- Widely adopted: its ubiquity means students can find supplementary resources, YouTube walkthroughs, and instructor materials at near-zero cost

**Weaknesses**
- Tones treated as a solved problem after Basics — no systematic perceptual discrimination drills within lessons, no multi-speaker exposure, no minimal-pair listening. A learner can pass exercises while still producing flat or wrong tones because the lesson exercises test reading comprehension, not tone accuracy
- Vocabulary load of 40-80 items per lesson is cognitively unsustainable without SRS. The book provides no retention mechanism between lessons; forgetting rates for items not drilled in context are high
- Dialogue situations are artificial and culturally thin: characters (Wang Peng, Gao Wenzhong, Bai Ying'ai) navigate contrived social scenarios (shopping for exact shoe sizes, asking teachers for homework extensions) that US learners rarely encounter and native speakers rarely phrase as scripted
- Grammar explanations are in English and metalinguistic — the approach implicitly teaches students to understand Chinese by first parsing it in English, building a translation reflex that fluent speakers must later unlearn
- Character introduction is topic-ordered, not frequency-ordered. High-frequency characters like 的/是/不/在 appear early but 从, 因为, 所以, 一下 appear scattered across lessons without a corpus rationale. Learners cannot predict which characters will unlock the most reading
- Stroke order is absent from the main textbook — it exists only in the separate character workbook, making it easy to skip. Research indicates writing reinforces reading recognition, so this separation has a real cost
- Series caps at approximately HSK 3 / Intermediate-Mid. The four-volume commitment takes two US academic years, and learners still cannot read an unadapted news article or follow fast native speech at completion
- No authentic input: audio is studio-recorded with single instructors reading at textbook pace. Multi-speaker, natural-speed, regionally-varied speech is entirely absent, so the jump from IC audio to real speech is a documented shock point for graduates
- Self-study hostile: the book explicitly depends on an instructor to model pronunciation, correct tone errors, and facilitate communicative practice. The exercises cannot substitute for human feedback on tones

**Borrow for xuexi**
- Front-load the full phonological map before vocabulary: IC's Basics section works. xuexi should ensure new users complete a tone and pinyin orientation (even 3-5 swipe cards) before the i+1 Feed begins, so the first graded sentence arrives with a phonological frame already in place — the Tone Dojo can serve this purpose if surfaced prominently at onboarding
- Topic-cluster vocabulary in early content: IC's first 5 lessons (greetings, family, time, hobbies, visiting) are a well-validated priority ordering for spoken Chinese. xuexi's HSK-graded feed should ensure this pragmatic cluster dominates early cards, not just raw HSK frequency rank
- The dual-mode text design (dialogue + narrative) maps well to xuexi's card types: short interpersonal sentences in the Feed can be balanced with slightly longer narrative sentences for reading register exposure — vary card genre, not just vocabulary
- Cumulative review architecture: IC's every-5-lesson 'Bringing It Together' is a manual version of what FSRS does automatically. xuexi already has this covered with FSRS, but the weekly recap Stat can explicitly surface 'you've now seen 50 characters 3+ times' to make the cumulative progress visible and emotionally rewarding
- Grammar pattern labeling without English explanation: IC labels patterns (Topic-Comment, Verb-Object Compound) and this vocabulary is useful. xuexi could show a one-line pattern tag on the gloss card ('Topic-Comment structure') that links to a minimal in-app reference, letting curious learners get the label without a full English grammar lecture

**Avoid for xuexi**
- English metalinguistic grammar explanations as primary learning mode: IC teaches grammar in English paragraphs. xuexi's stated model is comprehensible input — grammar should emerge from sentence exposure, not be front-loaded as English rules. If grammar notes appear at all, they should be optional, tappable, and minimal
- Single-speaker, studio-paced audio: IC's one-voice recordings are the direct cause of the 'textbook shock' graduates feel with native speech. xuexi already uses multi-speaker TTS (Qwen3-TTS) and the Tone Dojo has multi-speaker variability — this is a genuine structural advantage over IC. Protect and expand it; never normalize the Feed to a single voice
- 40-80 items per lesson as a vocabulary delivery unit: this is IC's most empirically problematic choice. xuexi's i+1 Feed with FSRS is the correct counter-model — introduce 1-3 new items per card in context, let FSRS space them, never batch 50 words without retention infrastructure
- Topic-ordered character introduction that ignores frequency: xuexi's HSK-graded vocabulary is already better aligned with frequency. Do not add curriculum units that force non-frequent characters early for 'situational completeness' the way IC introduces 贵姓 in Lesson 1
- Artificial dialogue scenarios: IC's contrived social situations are a known engagement killer. xuexi's Feed should source sentences from or model genuine register (informal speech, social media register, short functional texts) rather than scripted textbook exchanges
- Treating tones as done after initial instruction: IC introduces all four tones in Basics then largely abandons systematic tone training. xuexi's Tone Dojo is architecturally correct as an ongoing, high-variability, game-loop system — but it must remain a permanent part of the progression, not a one-time onboarding screen that users complete and exit
- Stroke-order instruction buried in a separate workbook: IC separates writing practice into an add-on book that most students deprioritize. If xuexi adds handwriting or stroke-order features, they must be integrated into the character-collection grid or review flow, not optional supplementary content — or explicitly out of scope

*Grounding: Moderately well-grounded. The following are web-verified from Cheng and Tsui publisher materials, Digmandarin review, Sishu Mandarin review, Migaku blog, and Studocu lesson documents: four-volume structure, ACTFL Five Cs framing, Basics section existence, Lessons 1-10 titles and topics, dual-dialogue-per-lesson format, Bringing It Together every 5 lessons, 40-80 word vocabulary load per lesson, stroke order absent from main textbook, HSK 3 ceiling, self-study hostility. ## New Practical Chinese Reader (Liu Xun, Beijing Language & Culture University) — Pedagogical Teardown

**Approach:** Hybrid grammar-communicative method combining audio-lingual drilling with dialogue-based communicative elements and explicit grammar instruction. Integrates structure, function, and cultural knowledge. Grammar-first with built-in communicative context (narrative around foreign students Gubo, Palanka, and Libo navigating China). Emphasizes systematic, deliberate character and tone mastery through repetitive drilling, graded progression, and cyclical review.

**Target level:** Beginner to intermediate (HSK 1-4 equivalent). Volume 1 targets absolute beginners; Volumes 2-3 intermediate-low; Volumes 4-6 intermediate-high. Design assumes classroom instruction (3 years, ~150 hours of contact); xuexi should recalibrate for self-paced, spaced-repetition delivery (likely 6-12 months for comparable depth depending on engagement).

**Method:** Dialogue-based narrative framework (story-driven around two foreign students' journey in China). Each lesson: dialogue text → vocabulary list → grammar notes → pronunciation/substitution drills → writing exercises with explicit stroke-order and character-component practice. Heavy use of audio materials and repetitive pattern drills (audio-lingual legacy). Grammar explanations provide explicit rules and functions. Hanzi learning emphasizes stroke order mastery and component recognition. Pinyin support gradual withdrawal: pinyin-heavy early (alongside characters), then character-dominant, eventually pinyin-only for reference in later volumes. Tones taught through dedicated drills with repetition and multi-speaker variation.

**Sequencing:** Two-part sequencing: (1) Intensive phonetics foundation: 2 preparatory lessons covering 21 initials, 38 finals, 4 tones with repetitive drills before main content. (2) Content progression across 70 lessons in 6 volumes: Vol 1-2 (50 lessons, ~1,000 vocab words) introduce basic sentence patterns, everyday dialogues, and ~700 characters via systematic component-based character introduction starting with ~14-60 easy base characters. Vol 3-4 add ~1,000 words covering history, medicine, sports, education. Vol 5-6 advance to essays and literary excerpts with 3,000+ total words. Character introduction follows: basic strokes → radicals/components → single-component characters → complex characters. Vocabulary graded; each lesson builds predictably on prior knowledge. Dialogue-heavy in early volumes transitions to reading-heavy in later volumes.

**Strengths**
- Authentic, natural dialogues grounded in realistic communicative scenarios (clothing, greetings, shopping, transportation)
- Systematic character pedagogy: component-based approach helps learners decode and remember new characters; stroke-order emphasis builds writing confidence and motor memory
- Comprehensive tone coverage: dedicated phonetics lessons (initials, finals, tones) with repetitive drills establish solid phonetic foundation before main content
- Balanced four-skill development: speaking, listening, reading, writing integrated throughout
- HSK-aligned vocabulary selection ensures relevance and standardization
- Cyclical review: constant revisiting of structures and functions across lessons builds retention
- Consistent lesson structure (text, vocab, grammar, drills, writing) aids predictability and metacognition
- Graded vocabulary load and grammar complexity: manageable progression from absolute beginner to intermediate (3 years)
- Cultural context woven into dialogues, not separated as 'culture lessons'
- Multiple reference materials: textbook, workbook, teacher guides, audio, video options

**Weaknesses**
- Grammar-heavy emphasis may overwhelm learners; metalinguistic explanations prioritized over intuitive comprehension
- Too much vocabulary per lesson for absolute beginners (reviewers note 'not ideal for beginners from scratch')
- Dialogue, while authentic, can feel stiff or overly formal in places; less natural variation in early volumes
- Insufficient 'practical' communication focus in later volumes; cultural content crowds out real-world conversation scenarios
- Character practice diminishes in later volumes (Vol 5-6); pinyin support withdrawn too aggressively, potentially leaving intermediate learners unable to reference pronunciation independently
- Limited grammatical depth/reasoning: explanations state rules but don't always clarify *why* structures are used or alternative forms
- Designed for sequential study; poor for reference or out-of-order topic learning
- No explicit comprehensible input (i+1) scaffolding: vocabulary load and grammar complexity sometimes jump without clear scaffolding transitions
- Workbook exercises are rote (fill-in-the-blank, repetition) rather than communicative or meaning-focused

**Borrow for xuexi**
- Systematic tone foundation: NPCR's 2-lesson phonetics prep validates xuexi's Tone Dojo focus; build progressive complexity (single tones → tone pairs → sentence tones) grounded in initials/finals structure
- Character-component approach: integrate component recognition (radicals, stroke patterns) into character-grid collection UI; teach learners which components recur so new hanzi feel less arbitrary
- Graded pinyin fade: replicate NPCR's gradual transition—start with pinyin + character, progressively reduce pinyin on known characters, keep pinyin available for reference
- Dialogue-narrative framing: anchor vocabulary/grammar lessons to episodic stories (characters' journey, relationships) rather than pure topic lists; mimics NPCR's Gubo/Palanka narrative engagement
- Cyclical review by function/structure: implement spaced-repetition review sets organized by grammar function (asking questions, giving opinions, describing) not just random card drill
- Prioritize authentic audio with multiple native speakers: NPCR's TTS (via Qwen3) should vary speaker, intonation, speed—train ears for real-world variation early
- Early lesson consistency: keep xuexi's Feed and Review structures predictable so learners build metacognitive routines (lower cognitive load, boost retention)
- HSK vocabulary alignment: prioritize HSK 1-3 in early content, HSK 4-6 in advanced—learners get certification-ready vocabulary, not arbitrary selections

**Avoid for xuexi**
- Explicit grammar-first trap: don't present grammar rules before meaningful input; xuexi's Feed should foreground comprehensible sentences with grammar emerging implicitly, not top-down rule-then-example
- Vocabulary overload per session: NPCR loads 20-30 words per lesson; xuexi's mobile context demands tighter i+1 bounds (5-10 new words per card batch) with more frequent, shorter review sessions
- Pinyin withdrawal without fallback: don't remove pinyin completely in intermediate content; keep pinyin toggle + mouseover/tap available always—xuexi's offline-first design can't rely on learners looking up externally
- Stiff, repetitive dialogue: xuexi's Feed should surface *varied* authentic sentences (TikTok-style diversity) not memorizable scripts; avoid dialogue-memorization traps
- Disconnected tone drills: Tone Dojo is great, but integrate tone practice into Feed (e.g., highlight tone challenges in context) so tones aren't isolated minigames but grounded in real words/phrases
- Character writing as centerpiece: xuexi is mobile-first; don't require stroke-order practice as progression gate; *offer* it, but don't block access to comprehension for non-writers
- Diminishing audio support: NPCR drops audio in Vol 5-6; xuexi's TTS should scale throughout *all* content, especially as text density increases—learners need audio crutch when prose gets dense
- Pure grammar categorization: avoid organizing Feed/Reviews purely by grammar pattern (subject-verb-object, question markers, etc.); prioritize *communication function* (greeting, asking for help, negotiating) so grammar feels purposeful
- No motivation layer: NPCR lacks gamification; xuexi's XP, streaks, combo meter are *ethical dopamine* levers—use them to motivate consistent, spaced review, not to encourage binge sessions or fake progress
- Ignoring character frequency: don't introduce characters uniformly; front-load high-frequency base characters (人, 大, 小, 口, 日, 木) that appear in 30-40% of common words—this compounds learning efficiency, unlike NPCR's component-first sequencing

## HSK Standard Course (Jiang Liping)

**Approach:** The course is best described as Structural-Situational, not pure communicative and not grammar-translation. Its design principle is explicit: "combining testing and teaching, promoting learning and teaching by testing." Vocabulary and grammar points are sourced directly from past HSK test papers, then embedded in scripted situational dialogues. This places it closer to the PPP (Present-Practice-Produce) model than to input-based or task-based learning. Characters are introduced systematically but secondary to pinyin at early levels. Tones get explicit treatment in the first two dedicated phonology lessons, then reappear via tone-collocation drills in later lessons. The approach is primarily deductive (rule given, then example) rather than inductive (input first, rule emerges).

**Target level:** Complete beginners (CEFR A1) at Level 1 through upper-intermediate (CEFR B2/C1) at Level 6. Primary audience is adult learners in formal classroom settings — university Mandarin courses, language schools, or HSK exam candidates. Self-study is possible but the design assumes teacher mediation for grammar scaffolding and communicative activities. The series is the dominant institutionally-adopted textbook globally for HSK preparation.

**Method:** Structural-Situational with test-alignment. Each lesson from L3 onward has a fixed 7-component structure: (1) Warm-up — pictures activate key vocabulary; (2) Text — three situational mini-dialogues, each 1-2 exchanges; (3) Notes — deductive grammar explanation using tables, described as 'concise and clear'; (4) Exercises — Q&A, picture description, sentence completion, group tasks; (5) Pinyin — tone-collocation drills for the lesson's disyllabic and trisyllabic words; (6) Characters — stroke order and radical decomposition; (7) Application — pair and group communicative tasks. Cultural notes appear only three times per level (Lessons 5, 10, 15 at Level 1), covering topics like age-asking norms, Chinese naming conventions, and communication tools. Audio is provided via QR code-linked files. The course is explicitly designed for classroom use with a teacher; the teacher's book contains step-by-step lesson plans and suggested teaching aids.

**Sequencing:** Level 1 has 15 lessons. Lessons 1-2 are dedicated entirely to phonology: all initials, finals, all four tones plus neutral tone, and basic syllabic structures — before any vocabulary is introduced. Lessons 3-15 each cover a situational topic: Lesson 3 (greetings/ni hao), Lesson 4 (thanks/xiexie), Lesson 5 (names), Lesson 6 (introductions, 'shi' copula), Lesson 7 (age/numbers), Lesson 8 (language ability, modal 'hui'), Lesson 9 (dates), Lesson 10 (food/drinks, 'xiang'), Lesson 11 (location/work, 'zai'), Lesson 12 (permission, 'neng'), Lesson 13 (time), Lesson 14 (weather), Lesson 15 (progressive aspect 'zai...ne'), then shopping (aspect marker 'le'), then transportation ('shi...de' construction). Grammar follows a clear frequency-first logic: copula 'shi' and possession 'you' come before aspect markers, which come before complex resultative compounds. Characters at Level 1: 17 basic strokes, 52 single-component characters, 15 radicals. Writing is limited to strokes and single-component characters; recognition extends to all ~150 Level 1 characters. Tone sequencing within lessons: Lessons 1-2 cover all tones in isolation; Lessons 6-15 progressively drill disyllabic tone combinations, then trisyllabic words, then neutral-tone words — mirroring how tones actually behave in connected speech. Total L1 vocabulary: exactly 150 words matching the HSK1 syllabus. Each of Levels 1-3 covers one volume; Levels 4-6 split into A and B volumes, for 9 volumes total. The lexical load grows roughly as: 150 (L1), 300 (L2), 600 (L3), 1200 (L4), 2500 (L5), 5000 (L6).

**Strengths**
- Front-loads all phonology in Lessons 1-2: learners have the full pinyin system and all four tones before encountering vocabulary, preventing tone-blindness early on.
- Tone collocation sequencing is linguistically sound: tones are drilled as disyllabic pairs first (the unit of natural speech), then trisyllabic words, then neutral-tone reductions — matching how tones actually surface in connected Chinese.
- Composite-word morphology awareness: because early vocabulary is chosen partly for combinatorial utility (e.g., 'gong zuo', 'shang ban', 'xia ban'), learners internalize morphemes that recombine at higher levels.
- Exact HSK syllabus coverage (150/300/600/1200/2500/5000 words per level) gives a clean scaffolding backbone usable even outside the textbook.
- Fixed 7-component lesson structure reduces cognitive load — learners always know where they are in a lesson.
- Grammar notes use tables with contrastive examples — efficient for the 3-4 grammar points per lesson.
- Abundant third-party resources (YouTube teacher walkthroughs, Udemy courses) have grown around the series, making gaps easier to fill.
- Characters introduced via radical decomposition and stroke order — teaches the internal logic of hanzi rather than rote memorization.
- Rapid early conversational payoff: by Lesson 6, learners can introduce themselves, state nationality, ask names, and discuss age — functional output within 5-6 hours of study.

**Weaknesses**
- Test-driven content selection distorts communicative authenticity: dialogues are written to contain test vocabulary, not to simulate real conversations. Learners pass HSK but often cannot use the language in unscripted situations.
- Artificial emotional register: every text is 'drenched in happiness' with inspirational moral lessons. No negative emotions, no conflict, no ordinary frustration — making the language feel inert and unmemorable.
- Grammar notes are too brief for self-study: designed to be expanded by a teacher. Reviewers note 'grammar explanations that in some cases are too short and lack examples.' Without a teacher, learners must supplement externally.
- Difficulty does not genuinely ramp: reviewers note the books 'just continue to expand grammar and add more words into the mix' without increasing cognitive demand or authentic complexity.
- Documented gaps: at least 19 grammar points on the official HSK 5 grammar list are absent from the Level 5 textbook — the test-alignment claim breaks down at higher levels.
- No audio-only workbook exercises: all practice is text-mediated. There is no listening-only drill format, which limits ear training for learners not in a classroom with a teacher modeling pronunciation.
- Vocabulary is not updated for contemporary usage: modern terms (messaging apps, social media, contemporary slang) are absent. The vocabulary set reads as institutionally curated, not living language.
- Tone instruction is isolated from meaning at the phonology stage: Lessons 1-2 teach tones on syllables without semantic anchoring, which reduces memorability. Learners who struggle with tones may not connect the drills to real words.
- Character writing load at Level 1 may not be appropriate for all learners: the course requires learning 17 strokes and writing single-component characters alongside phonology, vocabulary, and grammar — a heavy initial burden on mobile/self-directed learners.
- Cultural content is sparse and superficial: three cultural notes per level cover narrow topics, giving learners almost no insight into authentic contemporary Chinese social contexts.

**Borrow for xuexi**
- The two-lesson phonology front-load principle: xuexi's onboarding should dedicate focused early sessions to tone perception before significant vocabulary load, but anchor tones to real words (not naked syllables) so they are semantically grounded from the start.
- Disyllabic-first tone collocation sequencing: the Tone Dojo should prioritize disyllabic pairs at first (T2+T4, T3+T3, etc.) before moving to trisyllabic and sentence-level tone patterns. This matches the phonology of natural speech and the Standard Course's proven sequencing.
- Composite-word morpheme awareness: the comprehensible-input feed should surface morpheme-level annotations in the gloss (e.g., showing that 'gongzuo' = gong(work/labor) + zuo(do/make)), helping learners build productive vocabulary networks rather than isolated word memories.
- Radical decomposition in the character-collection grid: when a character is shown, display its radical and a few words sharing that radical. The Standard Course's stroke-to-radical-to-character progression is sound; xuexi can implement it visually in the character mastery grid.
- The 150/300/600/1200/2500 vocabulary milestone structure: use HSK level thresholds as explicit gamification milestones (level-up unlocks, streak celebrations) to give learners a shared external benchmark, since the HSK vocabulary lists are well-validated.
- The situational embedding principle for card sentences: every new vocabulary item in the comprehensible-input feed should appear in a micro-situation (who is speaking, to whom, in what context), not as a decontextualized example sentence. This is something the Standard Course attempts, even if its execution is artificial.
- Grammar tables as in-app gloss overlays: the Standard Course's use of compact contrastive tables for grammar points (e.g., 是...的 vs. 是...的 with different emphasis) is effective. Tapping a grammar point in xuexi should surface a compact table, not a wall of prose.

**Avoid for xuexi**
- Test-alignment as a content-selection principle: xuexi's feed vocabulary should be chosen for comprehensible-input value and communicative utility, not HSK test frequency. HSK grading is a scaffold, not a goal.
- Artificial emotional restriction on input content: xuexi's sentence feed must include emotional variety — confusion, humor, disappointment, surprise — to make language memorable and to model how Chinese is actually used. Happy-only content actively reduces retention by suppressing emotional salience.
- Classroom-dependency for grammar explanation: every grammar pattern surfaced in xuexi must have a complete self-contained explanation with at least three contrastive examples accessible in-app. The Standard Course's teacher-mediated model cannot be replicated on a mobile device.
- Isolating tones from meaning at the phonology stage: in the Tone Dojo and onboarding, tones should always be attached to real, semantically meaningful syllables (e.g., 'ma' meaning mother vs. hemp vs. horse vs. scold) rather than drilled on arbitrary syllables. Anchoring to meaning improves retention.
- Character writing as a prerequisite or early burden: at Level 1 on a mobile app, recognition-first is the correct priority. The Standard Course requires stroke-order writing early; xuexi should defer production writing entirely and focus on recognition and radical pattern awareness first.
- Workbook-style discrete exercises as the primary practice mechanic: fill-in-the-blank and sentence-completion are low-engagement on mobile. FSRS card reviews and the Tone Dojo replace these more effectively. The Standard Course's exercise types are designed for pen-and-paper classrooms.
- Content that lags contemporary Mandarin: xuexi should continuously refresh its sentence feed with current vocabulary domains (tech, social media, everyday informal register) — the Standard Course's institutionally curated vocabulary becomes stale quickly.
- Neutral emotional staging of all audio: the Standard Course uses a single-register studio voice. Xuexi's multi-speaker Qwen3-TTS is already superior; ensure variation in speaker gender, age, and emotional register across card audio to train recognition across real-world voice variation.

## Boya Chinese (Peking University Press) — Pedagogical Teardown

**Approach:** **Hybrid: Structure-Situation-Function Framework**

Boya uses a semi-communicative approach grounded in structural grammar but contextualized through real-life scenarios. The framework explicitly integrates: (1) linguistic structures (explicit grammar teaching), (2) situational contexts (campus life, daily interactions), and (3) communicative functions (how language is actually used). ## Remembering the Simplified Hanzi (Books 1 & 2) — Heisig & Richardson

**Approach:** Character-first mnemonic isolation. This is not grammar-translation, communicative, or input-based learning. It is a standalone visual-memory system: each character is assigned one English keyword, decomposed into named "primitive" components (radicals and sub-radical fragments), and bound together by an invented mnemonic story. The system makes no claim to teach Mandarin — it teaches only the meaning and written form of characters in deliberate isolation from language use. The theoretical bet is that decoupled character mastery creates a foundation for later language acquisition. The authors explicitly state in the introduction: "What you will not learn here is how to pronounce any of these characters or how to combine them to form new words."

**Target level:** Beginner to intermediate character-study as a standalone track. Most effective for: (a) heritage speakers who already speak Mandarin but cannot read or write; (b) learners who are simultaneously studying spoken Mandarin via another resource and want a parallel character-only system; (c) advanced learners who want to systematize gaps in their character knowledge. Least effective for: absolute beginners expecting a complete language course; learners with a deadline (travel, work) requiring conversational output; anyone expecting tones, pronunciation, or grammar.

**Method:** Mnemonic-primitive decomposition. Each character is parsed into 2–10 named sub-components ("primitives"), many of which overlap with the 214 Kangxi radicals but extend well beyond them. Each primitive receives a concrete English image (e.g., 日 = "sun," 月 = "moon," 心 = "heart"). A short narrative story weaves those images together into an absurd or vivid scene that encodes the character's English keyword. Example: 明 (bright/clear) = sun + moon, story: "The sun and the moon together make something bright." Invented etymologies are used freely whenever real ones are unhelpful. The authors acknowledge the fictions explicitly. Spaced repetition via Anki (community decks) is the dominant delivery mechanism for this system in practice, though the book itself predates widespread SRS adoption and uses paper drills. Tones: not mentioned. Pinyin: not included. Grammar: not mentioned. Vocabulary in context: not mentioned. Handwriting production is the explicit output skill targeted.

**Sequencing:** Component-complexity-first, frequency-second. The book starts with the smallest, simplest primitives (e.g., 一, 二, 三, 口, 日, 月) and systematically compounds them — a new component is introduced only after all characters buildable from existing components have been taught. Frequency is considered only as a tiebreaker. The consequence is severe: 不 (bù, one of the top-20 most frequent characters in any Chinese text) appears as character #924. The word "I/me" equivalent characters are delayed past lesson 22. Book 1 covers ~1,500 characters across 55 lessons, structured in three phases that progressively withdraw scaffolding: (1) Stories — lessons 1–12, full authored mnemonic narratives with handwritten-style fonts and stroke-by-stroke diagrams; (2) Plots — lessons 13–19, skeletal story outlines the learner must flesh out; (3) Elements — lessons 20–55, bare keyword + primitive labels, learner constructs stories unaided. Book 2 adds ~1,500 more characters to reach 3,000 — claimed to cover 99.2% of characters found in Chinese newspapers. The two-volume set can be studied sequentially or in parallel.

**Strengths**
- Primitive decomposition is genuinely clever: teaching characters as compositional structures (not arbitrary blobs) gives learners a mental grammar of visual form that transfers to unfamiliar characters. Over 80% of Chinese characters are phonosemantic compounds with consistent semantic radicals — the Heisig primitive system, however imperfect, maps onto this structure and reduces the cognitive load of distinguishing visually similar characters.
- Bottom-up ordering minimises interference: by ensuring every component of a new character has already been learned, the system avoids the 'black box' experience where a learner has no foothold on a character's structure.
- Scaffolded withdrawal (Stories → Plots → Elements) models good pedagogy in principle: explicit support is highest where complexity is lowest, and learners are progressively handed creative ownership. The mechanism itself is sound.
- Mnemonics become unnecessary once automaticity develops: the authors correctly note that the stories are scaffolding, not permanent crutches. This aligns with research on elaborative encoding — bizarre or vivid imagery accelerates initial encoding and the story can be forgotten once the form-meaning link is consolidated.
- Proven track record at scale: large Anki community decks (notably the 'Heisig RTH' deck) and many learner testimonials confirm 1,000–1,500 characters in 3–6 months is achievable with daily SRS practice.
- Works well as a supplement for heritage speakers: learners who already have spoken Mandarin in long-term memory gain enormous leverage from a character-form system — the phonetic and semantic knowledge pre-exists and the mnemonic simply supplies the written form.

**Weaknesses**
- No tones, no pronunciation, no pinyin — ever. The authors justify this by claiming Chinese characters are non-phonetic, which is the 'ideographic myth': over 80% of Chinese characters are phonosemantic compounds with a phonetic component that hints at (or historically determined) the syllable. The omission is not just an inconvenience — it actively creates a bad habit of treating hanzi as meaning-only symbols, which impedes natural reading (where phonological encoding is part of skilled comprehension).
- Frequency-blind sequencing creates months of zero communicative utility. A learner 300 characters in cannot read a menu, a street sign, or a WeChat message. The most common function words and verbs are buried deep because they are compositionally complex. This is a serious motivational and practical failure for most learners.
- Scaffold collapse at character ~500: the Stories section ends, and from lesson 20 onward the book is essentially a keyword-character list. Learners are told to invent their own stories, but without quality control, many construct weak or confusable mnemonics. Reviews consistently flag this as where learner dropout spikes.
- English keyword arbitrariness: the one keyword per character model forces oversimplification. 家 mapped to 'domicile' instead of 'home/family'; 好 mapped to 'good' (fine) but the character's usage range is much wider. Keywords can create false precision and later require unlearning. Chinese characters frequently carry multiple meanings that shift by context and compound — a single keyword erases this.
- No vocabulary in context: Chinese is overwhelmingly bisyllabic and morphologically compound — most meaning lives in word-pairs (e.g., 学习, 电话, 工作), not individual characters. Learning 3,000 characters in isolation without learning words means learners still cannot read, because they do not know which characters combine with which, in what order, or what those compounds mean.
- Prioritises a declining skill: handwriting production is the explicit output target. In 2025, the practical need to handwrite Chinese characters is minimal for most learners. Recognition, digital input (pinyin/stroke-based IME), and reading comprehension are far more relevant. The book does not address these.
- The 'learn characters first, then language' philosophy is pedagogically backwards for most learners and unsupported by SLA research. Comprehensible input research (Krashen, and later corpus-based studies) consistently shows that vocabulary and grammar acquisition is faster when embedded in meaningful, comprehensible communication from the start. Heisig's sequencing delays any meaningful input indefinitely.

**Borrow for xuexi**
- Primitive/component decomposition in the character-collection grid: when a learner taps a character in the mastery grid, xuexi should show that character's semantic and phonetic components with their own names and mnemonics. This gives learners a visual grammar of hanzi form — the single most transferable insight from Heisig. Each component should be a tappable node linking to other characters that share it.
- Scaffold-then-withdraw sequencing for new characters in the feed: for a character's first few exposures, the card should show the component breakdown and a short mnemonic image or story. After the FSRS algorithm confirms retention (say, 3 successful reviews), the scaffold drops and only the character appears. This mirrors the Stories → Plots → Elements arc but automated and per-character rather than per-book-section.
- Mnemonic story as an optional 'unlock' in FSRS reviews: after a learner fails a hanzi→meaning review twice, surface a component-based mnemonic story or illustration as a rescue cue. This uses elaborative encoding exactly where it is needed — at the moment of consolidation failure — rather than imposing it universally.
- Phonosemantic component display: Heisig ignores phonetic radicals; xuexi should actively exploit them. For the ~80% of characters with a phonetic component, the tap-to-gloss popover should label the phonetic component with its own pinyin and show 2–3 other characters that share it and the same (or similar) pronunciation. This builds implicit phonological pattern recognition that Heisig deliberately destroys.
- Visual confusable-pair flagging: the Heisig community has long mapped which characters get confused (e.g., 己/已/巳, 土/士, 末/未). xuexi's FSRS system should track error patterns and, when a learner confuses a pair, surface a comparison card that highlights the distinguishing component — borrowing the spirit of Heisig's 'distinctive features via story' approach but doing it reactively and visually.
- Progressive creative ownership: as a learner's mastery level rises (reflected in xuexi's character mastery grid), nudge them to write their own mnemonic note for a character in a free-text field. This harvests the self-generation effect — personally constructed encodings stick better than provided ones. This is Heisig's Plots/Elements philosophy implemented as a social or personal annotation layer.

**Avoid for xuexi**
- Tone-free character presentation: xuexi already has a Tone Dojo and pinyin toggle — maintain and deepen this. Never present a character without its tone marking available. The Heisig approach of treating hanzi as phonologically inert is empirically wrong and would undermine xuexi's audio-first feed. Every character in xuexi's FSRS deck and feed must be permanently coupled to its tone and pinyin.
- Frequency-blind sequencing: xuexi's HSK grading is the right call. Never reorder the content feed or review queue by component complexity at the expense of frequency. Learners need 不, 是, 我, 你, 的 in week one. A learner stuck doing component drills for months before hitting real function words will churn — this directly contradicts xuexi's stated ethic of anchoring engagement to genuine progress.
- Context-free character learning: xuexi's comprehensible-input feed is the direct antidote to Heisig's isolation problem. Never allow a character to appear only in isolation. Every FSRS review card should, at minimum, show the target character's most common 2-character word compound and a sample sentence, so the learner is always building vocabulary-in-context alongside form-meaning links.
- Keyword monosemy: avoid assigning a single English gloss as the permanent 'meaning' of a character. The tap-to-gloss popover should show the 2–3 most common translations and the key compound words (e.g., 好 → 'good; well; very' + 好的, 好吃, 爱好). Locking in one keyword as Heisig does creates fossilised misunderstandings that learners must pay later to correct.
- Scaffold-dropping without automation: the book's manual withdrawal of support at a fixed page is crude. xuexi's FSRS data knows exactly when a learner has consolidated a character — use that signal to automate scaffold removal per-character, not per-book-chapter. Some learners will need Stories-level support on character 1200; others will need it only for character 12.
- Handwriting as the primary output skill: xuexi is a mobile app. Stroke-order and handwriting production are not priorities. Invest in recognition, reading speed, and listening — skills that directly serve xuexi's comprehensible-input and audio-review loops. If stroke order is ever added, make it optional and framed as 'helps memory encoding' rather than a core competency.
- The 'characters-first, language-later' philosophy: xuexi's feed integrates characters inside i+1 sentences with native TTS audio from day one. Never create a mode or onboarding flow that tells learners to finish a character set before engaging with real content. Comprehensible input and character acquisition should be concurrent and mutually reinforcing from lesson one.

## Mandarin Companion & Chinese Breeze Graded Readers Teardown

**Approach:** **Pedagogical Method: Input-Based Extensive Reading (NOT Grammar-Translation or Traditional Communicative)**

Both series employ **comprehensible-input methodology** centered on extensive reading—consuming high volumes of engaging, level-appropriate text for natural acquisition rather than explicit grammar instruction. Mandarin Companion reimagines Western classics (Sherlock Holmes, Emma, Journey to the Center of the Earth) in Chinese settings; Chinese Breeze offers original stories, mysteries, and folklore—both avoiding artificiality of translated classics.

**Core principle:** Learners acquire vocabulary and grammar patterns naturally when encountering them repeatedly in context at the ~95%+ comprehensibility threshold. Neither series is communicative in the traditional sense (dialogue-based, interaction-focused); both are receptive/passive.

**Character-First Presentation, Vocabulary-Controlled Sequencing:**
The graded readers present characters in authentic prose, not isolated stroke-by-stroke instruction. However, vocabulary selection follows frequency order (HSK alignment), ensuring high-frequency characters appear first. Mandarin Companion explicitly controls unique character count: Breakthrough ~150, Level 1 ~300, Level 2 ~450 (mapped to HSK 1→4). Chinese Breeze uses word count instead: 300, 500, 750, 1,100 words across four levels.

**Target level:** HSK 1–4 (Beginner to Low-Intermediate); Xuexi positioned as HSK 1–6+ with extended scope.

**Method:** **Target Learners & Levels:**
- Mandarin Companion: HSK 1–4 (complete beginner through low-intermediate); both simplified and traditional character editions
- Chinese Breeze: HSK 1–4 (same); published by Peking University Press, widely adopted in universities; professional narration

**How Tones & Hanzi Are Handled:**

*Tones:* Neither series explicitly teaches tones—both assume learners already know pinyin/tone marks. They rely on **native speaker audio** as the primary tone resource. Research shows ear training (tone discrimination exercises, minimal pairs) is prerequisite for production; learners can distinguish tones within 2–4 weeks with consistent audio immersion. Graded readers support this passively: audio recurrence and pitch patterns reinforce tone recognition, but no dedicated tone pedagogy or gamified discrimination practice.

*Hanzi:* Both present characters in running prose (no stroke-order breakdown). Pinyin support varies:
  - Mandarin Companion: Available in simplified/traditional; pinyin typically as glossary or footnotes, NOT always toggleable
  - Chinese Breeze: Pinyin in footnotes; **annotations always visible** (cannot be hidden for advanced readers)
  
   This is a **critical weakness**: advancing readers are forced to read pinyin, breaking automaticity and creating cognitive load.

**Vocabulary Recycling Strategy:**
Both intentionally reuse high-frequency words across stories. Mandarin Companion uses the same ~300 characters repeatedly across ~10k-word books; Chinese Breeze does the same at its word count level. This creates natural spaced repetition—vocabulary recurs spread over weeks of reading, strengthening retention without flashcard tedium.

**Sequencing:** **Mandarin Companion Levels:**
- **Breakthrough (~150 unique characters):** "The Prince and the Pauper," "Erta's Promise" | ~HSK 1
- **Level 1 (~300 unique characters, ~10k total):** "Sherlock Holmes and the Case of the Curly-Haired Company," "Emma," "The Secret Garden," "The Country of the Blind" | ~HSK 2–3
- **Level 2 (~450 unique characters, ~10k total):** "Journey to the Center of the Earth," additional titles | ~HSK 3–4

**Chinese Breeze Levels:**
- **Level 1 (300 words):** ~21 titles including "Wrong, Wrong, Wrong!" | ~8k characters per book
- **Level 2 (500 words):** 7 titles, e.g. "An Old Painting"
- **Level 3 (750 words):** 5–6 titles, e.g. "The Moon Sculpture Left Behind"
- **Level 4 (1,100 words):** 5 titles | Ceiling; no higher levels published

**Character Introduction Sequence Rationale:**
Both follow **frequency-based ordering** (high-frequency characters first). Academic research confirms: the top 300 characters comprise 64% of everyday Chinese text; knowing 1,000 covers ~90% of written materials. HSK 1 includes 82 characters (most essential daily communication); HSK 4 requires 1,064 characters total. Neither series explicitly publishes character-by-character acquisition order, but vocabulary selection ensures frequent characters recur 10–30+ times across a book (Mandarin Companion design principle: learners need 10–30 encounters to internalize a word).

**Strengths**
- **Engagement through narrative:** Adapted classics (Emma, Sherlock Holmes, Journey to the Center of the Earth) and diverse genres (mystery, romance, folklore) are intrinsically more motivating than grammar-focused textbooks; learners read for plot, not obligation.
- **Comprehensibility threshold maintained:** Careful vocabulary control keeps comprehension at ~95%+, enabling fluent reading and inference of unknown words from context—the 'sweet spot' for acquisition.
- **Natural, distributed vocabulary exposure:** Intentional word repetition (10–30+ encounters per book) creates incidental learning superior to massed study; research shows spaced repetition via reading achieves 85% retention vs. 35% for cramming.
- **Native speaker audio:** Chinese Breeze includes professionally narrated audio; Mandarin Companion offers Audible versions. Audio input is critical for tone perception and speaking rhythm—graded readers avoid artificial, slow TTS.
- **Frequency-aligned sequencing:** Both align with HSK vocabulary order and character frequency research (top 300 chars = 64% of text), ensuring learners encounter high-yield characters first.
- **Low-cost, accessible:** Print and e-book formats; $7–13 per book; Mandarin Companion provides sample chapters online (PDF).
- **Proven efficacy for extensive reading:** Longitudinal studies confirm extensive reading improves fluency, comprehension, character recognition, and writing—particularly when texts are appropriately leveled.

**Weaknesses**
- **No explicit tone pedagogy:** Both assume tone knowledge is already acquired. Tones are acquired passively through audio, not trained actively (no tone-discrimination drills, minimal pairs games, or multi-speaker variability exercises). Xuexi's Tone Dojo (rapid tone-ID game with multi-speaker variability + haptics) directly fills this gap.
- **Pinyin is not toggleable:** Mandarin Companion's pinyin is often fixed in glossaries; Chinese Breeze shows pinyin in footnotes at all levels. Advanced learners are forced to re-read pinyin, breaking character recognition automaticity and creating cognitive load. No scaffolding from pinyin→hanzi-only progression.
- **Text difficulty assessment is unreliable:** Teachers and publishers frequently underestimate text difficulty; comprehension falling below 95% (80–90%) fragments meaning, breaking engagement and reducing incidental learning efficiency.
- **Vocabulary retention depends on contextualized activation:** Extensive reading alone does NOT guarantee vocabulary sticks. Research shows Chinese learners often fail to use contextualized activation and management strategies—words read but not actively internalized. Without spaced-repetition follow-up, retention is inconsistent.
- **No vocabulary tracking or mastery feedback:** Learners don't know which words they've internalized vs. which need review. No character-collection grid or mastery visualization (contrast: xuexi provides character-grid stats showing mastery progression).
- **Ceiling at HSK 4 (intermediate):** Both series stop abruptly—no advanced content beyond 1,100-word Chinese Breeze Level 4. Learners seeking upper-intermediate+ content must switch resources entirely.
- **Chinese Breeze outdated HSK alignment:** Built for HSK 2.0; not aligned with HSK 3.0 (published 2021, reflects modern vocabulary usage). Mandarin Companion is more timeless due to classic-adaptation approach.
- **No spaced-repetition system:** Graded readers rely on incidental recurrence within a book. Once a book is finished, vocabulary exposure stops. No scheduled review schedule (like Anki or xuexi's FSRS) to combat forgetting curve.
- **Assumes reading-first, not listening-first:** Both assume literacy in pinyin/characters before reading. Early absolute beginners with zero Chinese may be overwhelmed—no scaffolding from pure-audio tones/listening comprehension first.
- **Limited genre breadth at low levels:** Mandarin Companion Breakthrough/Level 1 offerings are sparse (few titles to sustain long-term extensive reading habit); Chinese Breeze Level 1 has only ~6 titles before difficulty jumps.
